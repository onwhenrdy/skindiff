## Coverage
This generation of reviews focuses on the post-2020 regulatory landscape:
- The EU's pivot to "New Approach Methodologies" (NAMs) for cosmetic, biocide, and pesticide safety assessment after the cosmetic-animal-testing ban.
- Integration of QSAR, in-vitro permeation (TG 428), and PBPK modelling into IATA (Integrated Approaches to Testing and Assessment).
- The role of mechanistic 1D diffusion modelling (e.g. tools like skindiff, Cosmologic CosmoTherm-Skin, Simulations Plus DermSim) in bridging in-vitro Kp to in-vivo systemic exposure.
- Critical evaluation of published QSAR models for use in regulatory dossiers.
- Defined Approaches (DAs) for skin sensitization (less directly relevant to permeation).

## Key takeaways
1. Regulatory acceptance of in silico methods has matured: QSAR + mechanistic-diffusion + PBPK pipelines are now considered acceptable for screening-level risk assessment under REACH and EU cosmetics regulation, though not yet for definitive decisions on novel high-priority substances.
2. The community has converged on a layered approach: QSAR for triage, in-vitro Franz cell for refined Kp, mechanistic-diffusion to translate Kp to dose-realistic exposure, PBPK to translate to systemic concentrations.
3. Open and reproducible tools are favoured over proprietary black boxes for regulatory purposes. The lack of an open-source, validated, well-documented skin-diffusion simulator has been called out as a gap.
4. Better data is the persistent ask — newer in-vitro permeation databases under development (Cosmetics Europe, Hartung lab CITER) aim to provide consistent, well-controlled data that can lift QSAR ceilings.
