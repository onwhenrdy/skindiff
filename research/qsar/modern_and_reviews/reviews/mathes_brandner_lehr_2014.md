# Mathes, Brandner, Lehr 2014 — In vitro skin models for permeation studies

## Citation
Mathes SH, Ruffner H, Graf-Hausner U. "The use of skin models in drug development." Advanced Drug Delivery Reviews 2014; 69-70: 81-102. DOI: 10.1016/j.addr.2013.12.006.

(Note: "Brandner / Lehr" likely refers to a related but separate review by Lehr's group at Saarland — Lehr CM was prolific on epidermal models around 2014. Possible match: Brandner JM. Eur J Pharm Biopharm 2009; 72: 289 on skin barrier function. The user-provided "Mathes/Brandner/Lehr 2014" may conflate two distinct reviews; verify.)

## Coverage
The Mathes et al. 2014 review covers:
- Excised human and animal skin (Franz diffusion cell standard).
- Reconstructed human epidermis (RHE) — EpiSkin, EpiDerm, SkinEthic, Phenion FT — and full-thickness reconstructed skin.
- 2D keratinocyte cultures and 3D organotypic skin equivalents.
- Validation status for OECD TG 431 (corrosion), 439 (irritation), 428 (in vitro permeation).
- Comparison of permeation flux measured in RHE versus excised human skin — typically 2-50x higher in RHE due to looser SC.
- Suitability for QSAR data generation: which models give reproducible permeation data, which give matched lipid composition, which match human in vivo.

## Key takeaways
1. Excised human skin is the gold standard for in-vitro permeation but is heterogeneous (donor-to-donor variation can be 5-10x for the same compound).
2. Reconstructed human epidermis is more reproducible but systematically more permeable than human skin; correction factors are compound-specific and not always available. Useful for ranking, less useful for absolute Kp.
3. Animal skin (porcine, rodent) varies in suitability — porcine ear is the closest human surrogate; rodent skin is generally too permeable.
4. The data quality of any QSAR is fundamentally limited by the quality of the underlying in-vitro permeation measurements. Heterogeneity in skin source, vehicle, occlusion, and Franz-cell geometry across labs is the dominant noise source — bigger than typical analytical measurement error.
