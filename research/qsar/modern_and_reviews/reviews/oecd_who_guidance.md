# OECD and WHO guidance documents on dermal absorption

## Citation
Three key documents:

1. **OECD Test Guideline 428** (2004): "Skin Absorption: In Vitro Method." Defines the standard Franz-cell in-vitro permeation experiment. https://www.oecd.org/env/test-no-428-skin-absorption-in-vitro-method-9789264071087-en.htm

2. **OECD Guidance Document 28** (2004): "Guidance Document for the Conduct of Skin Absorption Studies." Companion document to TG 428. ENV/JM/MONO(2004)2.

3. **OECD Guidance Notes 156** (2011): "Guidance Notes on Dermal Absorption." ENV/JM/MONO(2011)36. The most comprehensive — covers in-vitro, in-vivo, default values, and how to use QSAR predictions in risk assessment. Free download from OECD iLibrary.

4. **OECD Guidance Document 47** (2017, updated): "Guidance for the conduct and design of chronic toxicity and carcinogenicity studies, supporting test guideline 451, 452 and 453." Includes dermal exposure considerations (less relevant for QSAR-only users).

5. **WHO EHC 235** (2006): Dermal Absorption. Environmental Health Criteria 235, IPCS / WHO. https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9241572353

## Coverage
These documents define:
- Acceptable in-vitro permeation experimental designs (TG 428).
- Quality criteria for raw permeation data (mass balance, recovery, integrity test).
- How to extract Kp, J_ss, and absorbed fractions from raw data.
- When QSAR predictions can substitute for measured Kp (typically: read-across between structurally similar compounds; not as a sole basis for risk assessment of a new chemical).
- Default skin absorption values for risk assessment in the absence of data (e.g. 100% for compounds < 500 Da and logP between -1 and 4; 10% otherwise — REACH defaults).
- Rabbit / human / pig surrogate suitability and correction factors.

## Key takeaways
1. For regulated risk assessment, in-vitro Kp data from TG 428-compliant experiments is the standard. QSAR is allowed for read-across but not as the sole evidence.
2. The WHO EHC 235 document provides the most thorough single-source discussion of the science underlying dermal-absorption risk assessment.
3. Default-value tables (e.g. 100% absorption for small/lipophilic; 10% otherwise) are the regulatory backstop when no data exists. QSAR + mechanistic modelling can refine these defaults.
4. EFSA and ECHA reference these OECD documents in their dermal-absorption risk-assessment workflows for pesticides and industrial chemicals respectively.
